SOLARIS BATTERY CHEMISTRY — 18-MONTH CELL STUDY
Research summary and internal notes
Helvern Institute for Energy Storage, Applied Cells Group
Principal investigator: Dr. Lena Vasquez
Study window: January 2024 - June 2025 (18 months)
Report reference: HI-ACG-2025-114


1. BACKGROUND AND OBJECTIVE

Solaris is a sodium-ion battery chemistry developed by the Applied Cells Group,
pairing a polyanionic sodium-vanadium-phosphate cathode (internally designated
NX-3) with a hard-carbon anode and a fluoroethylene-carbonate-modified
electrolyte. The objective of this study was to characterize the Solaris NX-3
chemistry against a commercial lithium-ion NMC 811 baseline across energy
density, cycle life, temperature tolerance, fast-charge behavior, safety
margin, and projected cost, at a maturity sufficient to justify (or kill) a
pilot manufacturing line.

The headline question posed by the steering committee: can Solaris reach at
least 80 percent of NMC energy density at no more than 70 percent of NMC cost,
while beating NMC on cold-weather performance and safety?

Context. This is the third generation of the program. NX-1 (2021) validated
the polyanionic cathode concept at coin-cell scale but delivered only
118 Wh/kg. NX-2 (2022-2023) reached 205 Wh/kg in single-layer pouches but
suffered rapid impedance growth above 40C, traced to electrolyte oxidation at
the cathode surface. NX-3 addresses this with a 2 percent
fluoroethylene-carbonate electrolyte additive and an alumina surface coating
on the cathode particles, applied by atomic layer deposition. The hard-carbon
anode is unchanged from NX-2 except for a 6 percent increase in pressing
density. Sodium-ion remains attractive for the same structural reasons as at
program start: no lithium, nickel, or cobalt exposure; aluminum current
collectors on both electrodes (copper eliminated entirely); and the ability
to ship cells at true 0 volts, which removes a class of transport
restrictions that apply to lithium-ion.


2. METHODOLOGY

Cell population. The study used 240 pouch cells of the Solaris NX-3 chemistry,
each of 18 Ah nominal capacity, drawn from three production batches to capture
batch-to-batch variance. A control population of 60 commercial NMC 811 pouch
cells was cycled under identical protocols.

Temperature conditions. Cells were divided across three thermal chambers held
at -20C, 25C, and 45C. Chamber temperatures were logged at 1-minute intervals
and held within +/-0.5C.

Charge protocols. Two charge rates were tested at each temperature: a C/2
standard charge and a 2C fast charge, both with constant-current
constant-voltage termination at 4.05 V. Discharge was 1C to a 2.0 V cutoff.
Cycling continued until a cell reached 80 percent of its initial measured
capacity, the industry-standard end-of-life definition, or until the study
window closed.

Instrumentation. Capacity, coulombic efficiency, DC internal resistance
(measured by 10-second pulse), and cell swelling (laser micrometer) were
recorded every 25 cycles. Twelve cells were sacrificed at intervals for
post-mortem analysis: electrode cross-sections by SEM, electrolyte
decomposition by GC-MS.

Formation and screening. All cells completed a 3-cycle formation protocol at
C/10 and 30C before entering the study. Cells outside +/-2 percent of nominal
capacity or +/-10 percent of median DC resistance after formation were
rejected; the rejection rate was 4.6 percent for Solaris cells and 1.7 percent
for the commercial NMC controls, consistent with the maturity gap between a
lab-scale line and a commercial one.

Safety testing. A separate lot of 24 cells underwent accelerating-rate
calorimetry (ARC) and nail-penetration testing to establish thermal-runaway
onset temperatures.

Statistics. Cycle-life figures are reported as medians with interquartile
ranges; group comparisons use the Mann-Whitney U test at a 0.01 significance
level. All raw cycler data, chamber logs, and analysis notebooks are archived
under report reference HI-ACG-2025-114 in the institute data vault.


3. KEY FINDINGS

3.1 Energy density. Solaris NX-3 cells delivered a measured energy density of
285 Wh/kg at the cell level, averaged across all three batches (standard
deviation 6 Wh/kg). This compares to 190 Wh/kg typical of first-generation
sodium-ion cells and falls 18 percent short of the 348 Wh/kg measured on the
NMC 811 control cells. The result clears the steering committee's 80-percent
threshold.

3.2 Cycle life. At 25C with the C/2 charge protocol, Solaris cells reached
4,200 cycles to 80 percent capacity retention (median; interquartile range
3,950-4,410). The NMC control reached 1,800 cycles under the same protocol.
At 45C, Solaris degradation accelerated: median cycle life fell to 2,900
cycles, driven primarily by electrolyte decomposition confirmed in post-mortem
GC-MS. The 2C fast-charge protocol at 25C reduced Solaris cycle life by
roughly 12 percent versus C/2 — a notably small fast-charge penalty.

3.3 Low-temperature performance. At -20C, Solaris cells retained 88 percent
of their 25C discharge capacity, versus 61 percent for the NMC control. DC
internal resistance at -20C rose 2.1x from its 25C value, versus 4.8x for
NMC. This is the chemistry's most commercially distinctive result and supports
targeting cold-climate stationary storage and delivery-fleet applications
first.

3.4 Fast charging. Under the 2C protocol at 25C, Solaris cells charged from
10 percent to 80 percent state of charge in 22 minutes with no observable
lithium-plating analog (sodium plating) in post-mortem SEM up to 1,000 cycles.

3.5 Cost. A bottom-up cost model using the study's bill of materials, at an
assumed 5 GWh/year production scale, projects a cost per kWh of $61 for
Solaris cell production, versus $94 per kWh for the NMC 811 baseline under
identical assumptions. The largest savings come from the absence of nickel and
cobalt and from aluminum current collectors on both electrodes. Sensitivity:
a 50 percent rise in vanadium price moves Solaris to $68 per kWh.

3.6 Safety margin. ARC testing measured thermal-runaway onset at 212C for
Solaris NX-3, versus 168C for the NMC control — a 44C wider safety margin.
Peak heat-release rate during nail penetration was 3.4x lower than NMC. No
Solaris cell exhibited fire or ejection in any abuse test; two of six NMC
control cells did.


3.7 Coulombic efficiency and swelling. Mean coulombic efficiency at 25C
stabilized at 99.91 percent after formation (NMC control: 99.95 percent).
Cell swelling at end-of-test was 3.1 percent of initial thickness at 25C and
5.8 percent at 45C, both within the 8 percent pack-design allowance. DC
internal resistance grew 31 percent over the first 3,000 cycles at 25C, with
most of the growth in the first 500 cycles, consistent with SEI stabilization
on the hard-carbon anode.

3.8 Batch-to-batch variance. Median cycle life across the three production
batches spanned 4,050 to 4,320 cycles, and energy density spanned
281-291 Wh/kg. Batch B, produced after a calendering roller replacement,
showed the tightest distributions - evidence that the remaining variance is
process-driven rather than intrinsic to the chemistry, which is encouraging
for manufacturing transfer.


4. DISCUSSION

Taken together, the results position Solaris NX-3 not as a universal
lithium-ion replacement but as a targeted winner in two segments. First,
cold-climate commercial fleets: the 88 percent capacity retention at -20C,
combined with the small fast-charge penalty, addresses the single largest
complaint of northern-latitude delivery operators. Second, stationary
storage, where the $61 per kWh projected cost and the 4,200-cycle life
compound: levelized cost of storage modeling (appendix C of the full report)
puts Solaris 34 percent below the NMC baseline per delivered kWh over a
20-year deployment. The 18 percent energy-density deficit against NMC 811
matters little in stationary applications and is tolerable in delivery vans;
it likely rules out passenger-car range competition for this generation.

The safety results deserve emphasis in partner conversations: a 212C runaway
onset with no fire or ejection in any abuse test materially simplifies pack
design - reduced inter-cell barriers, simpler venting - which the cost model
does not yet credit. A pack-level cost study should follow the pilot line
decision.


5. LIMITATIONS

- Form factor. All results are from 18 Ah pouch cells. No automotive-format
  pack-level validation has been performed; pack integration effects
  (compression, cooling, busbar losses) are unquantified.
- Study duration. The 18-month window is short relative to the 8-to-10-year
  service life expected in automotive and stationary deployments. Calendar
  aging, as distinct from cycle aging, is not fully characterized; a shelved
  cell cohort continues under observation.
- Supply chain. The hard-carbon anode material came from a single supplier
  (batch-qualified, but single-source). Anode variability across suppliers is
  an open risk for the cost model and for cycle-life reproducibility.
- Statistical power at -20C. The cold chamber held 48 cells; wider confidence
  intervals apply to all -20C figures.
- The cost model assumes 5 GWh/year scale that does not yet exist for this
  chemistry; first-plant costs will be materially higher.


6. FUTURE WORK

1. Cylindrical format transfer. Re-validate the chemistry in the 21700
   cylindrical format in H2 2026, since two prospective partners require
   cylindrical cells.
2. Pilot line. Proceed with the proposed 50 MWh pilot manufacturing line,
   targeted to commission in 2027, contingent on board approval in the
   Q4 2026 capital review.
3. Calendar-aging cohort. Continue the shelved-cell study to 36 months and
   publish an interim calendar-aging model in mid-2027.
4. Automotive partner. Advance the evaluation agreement with Meridian Motors
   (fictional automaker) covering pack-level winter trials of 88 kWh Solaris
   packs in delivery vans.
5. Second anode source. Qualify a second hard-carbon supplier before pilot
   line commissioning to de-risk the single-source dependency noted above.


7. COMMITTEE RECOMMENDATION

Proceed. The chemistry clears all three steering-committee gates: energy
density (285 Wh/kg, above the 278 Wh/kg gate), cost ($61 per kWh, below the
$66 gate), and differentiated cold-weather and safety performance. Primary
risks are calendar aging and anode supply, both carried as tracked risks with
mitigation owners assigned.

Gate review detail. The committee voted 6-1 to proceed; the dissenting vote
cited the single-source anode risk and asked that second-supplier
qualification (item 5 above) be made a blocking precondition for pilot-line
commissioning rather than a parallel workstream. The chair accepted this
amendment. Budget approved for the next phase: $14.2M for the cylindrical
transfer program and $3.1M for the extended calendar-aging cohort, drawn from
the FY2026 applied-research envelope. The pilot line capital request
(estimated $120M) goes to the board in the Q4 2026 capital review with a
go/no-go informed by the H2 2026 cylindrical results.
